Ratios & Proportions

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::ratios_proportions · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs fractions, percents · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Simplify ratios and distinguish part-to-part from part-to-whole.
  • Model a ratio with a common multiplier (the 'parts' method).
  • Solve proportions by cross-multiplication and apply scaling.
  • Combine two ratios that share a term.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Equivalent fractions and dividing a quantity.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Simplify the ratio 18:24.3:4.
  2. If a:b = 2:3 and a = 10, find b.15.
  3. Split 30 in the ratio 2:3.12 and 18.
  4. Solve 3/4 = x/20.x = 15.
  5. A recipe uses 2 cups flour to 3 cups sugar. Scale to 6 cups flour.Multiply both by 3 → 6:9.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves simplifying, scaling, and cross-multiplication — the exact moves needed for the parts method and combined ratios today.
If students struggle: If splitting 30 as 2:3 is hard, show 2+3 = 5 parts, 30/5 = 6 per part, then 12 and 18.

Prior-knowledge bridge

A ratio is a pair of equivalent fractions waiting to be scaled. In the Do-Now you scaled 2:3 by a multiplier; today you'll formalize that into the 'parts' method — sum the parts, find the value of one part, then scale every share — and chain ratios that share a term.

Learning intention

By the end you can solve ratio and proportion problems with the parts method, scale with cross-multiplication, and combine ratios sharing a common term.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can turn a ratio into parts and find the value of one part.
  • I can solve a:b problems given a difference or a total.
  • I can combine a:b and b:c into a:c.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
The ratio of boys to girls in a class is 3:5. If there are 32 students in total, how many are girls?
  • A12
  • B15
  • C18
  • D20
  • E24

Think-aloud

  1. The ratio 3:5 means the class is made of 3 + 5 = 8 equal parts.
  2. 32 students ÷ 8 parts = 4 students per part.
  3. Girls are 5 parts: 5 × 4 = 20.
  4. Check: boys = 3 × 4 = 12, and 12 + 20 = 32. ✓

Correct: D. Total parts = 3 + 5 = 8; each part = 32/8 = 4; girls = 5 × 4 = 20.

Takeaway. Parts method: value of one part = total ÷ (sum of ratio terms); then scale each share.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
If a : b = 4 : 7 and b = 21, what is a?
  • A9
  • B12
  • C14
  • D15
  • E28
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The 21 matches the '7' in the ratio.
  2. One part = 21 ÷ 7 = 3.
  3. a = 4 parts = 4 × 3.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — a = 12.
If incorrect: Find the value of one part first (21/7 = 3), then multiply by a's term (4).

Answer: B. b corresponds to 7 parts, so one part = 21/7 = 3; then a = 4 × 3 = 12.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Solve for x: 5/8 = x/40.
  • A8
  • B15
  • C20
  • D25
  • E64
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Cross-multiply: 8 · x = 5 · 40.
  2. 8x = 200.
  3. x = 200/8.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — x = 25.
If incorrect: Cross-multiply both diagonals and solve the resulting equation.

Answer: D. Cross-multiply: 8x = 5 × 40 = 200, so x = 25.

A map's scale is 1 cm : 5 km. Two cities are 8.5 cm apart on the map. How far apart are they in reality?
  • A13.5 km
  • B40 km
  • C42.5 km

Answer: C. Each cm is 5 km, so 8.5 × 5 = 42.5 km.

  • B18

Answer: C. Cats − dogs = 5 − 3 = 2 parts = 16, so one part = 8; dogs = 3 × 8 = 24.

If a : b = 2 : 3 and b : c = 4 : 5, what is a : c?
  • A2 : 5
  • B8 : 15
  • C1 : 2
  • D6 : 5
  • E10 : 9
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: B. Scale to a common b (LCM of 3 and 4 is 12): a:b = 8:12 and b:c = 12:15, so a:c = 8:15.
