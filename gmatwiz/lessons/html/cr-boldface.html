Critical Reasoning: Boldface (Describe the Role)

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::boldface · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify the author's conclusion, distinguish evidence, opposing views, and conclusions · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Determine the author's overall conclusion first.
  • Label each boldfaced portion by its role: conclusion, supporting evidence, opposing position, or objection.
  • Match both labels to the answer choice that describes them.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Telling an author's own conclusion from a view the author opposes.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes.

  1. What roles can a boldfaced statement play?Conclusion, supporting evidence, an opposing position, or an objection the author answers.
  2. First step on a boldface question?Find the author's own conclusion.
  3. If a portion is a view the author argues against, its role is...An opposing position (not the author's conclusion).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls the small set of roles a boldfaced portion can play and the 'find the conclusion first' method.
If students struggle: Have them mark each boldface as 'author agrees' or 'author disagrees' before labeling.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Boldface questions are structure questions: they ask what job each bolded sentence does. If you first pin down the author's own conclusion, every other sentence becomes easy to label as support, an opposing view, or an objection the author rebuts.

Learning intention

By the end you can label each boldfaced portion by its role in the argument and match both to the correct answer choice.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can state the author's overall conclusion.
  • I can label a boldface as support, opposing view, or objection.
  • I can match both role labels to the answer choice.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
Analyst: <b>The company should keep its downtown store open.</b> Critics point to the store's high rent. However, <b>the downtown store attracts customers who then shop at the company's other locations.</b> In the analyst's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is the conclusion the analyst defends; the second is evidence the analyst offers in support of that conclusion.
  • BThe first is an objection the analyst rejects; the second is the analyst's conclusion.
  • CBoth portions are evidence for the analyst's conclusion.
  • DThe first is the conclusion; the second is an objection to it.
  • EThe first is evidence; the second is the analyst's conclusion.

Think-aloud

  1. Find the author's conclusion: 'keep the downtown store open.' That's the first bold portion.
  2. The critics' rent point is an objection (not bolded).
  3. The second bold portion answers that objection - it's supporting evidence.
  4. So first = conclusion, second = support -> choice A.

Correct: A. The analyst's own view is 'keep the store open' (conclusion). The second bold portion (it draws customers to other stores) is support answering the rent objection. So first = conclusion, second = supporting evidence.

Takeaway. Find the author's conclusion first; then every boldface is either that conclusion, support for it, or a view the author opposes.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
Columnist: Many believe that <b>working longer hours always increases a company's output.</b> But <b>studies show that overworked employees make more errors, which lowers total output.</b> The belief is therefore mistaken. In the columnist's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is a position the columnist argues against; the second is evidence used to reject that position.
  • BThe first is the columnist's conclusion; the second supports it.
  • CBoth portions are the columnist's evidence.
  • DThe first is evidence; the second is the conclusion.
  • EThe first is the conclusion; the second is an objection to it.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. What does the columnist actually conclude?
  2. Is the first bold portion something the columnist agrees or disagrees with?
  3. Label the second: does it support the belief or attack it?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - first = opposed position, second = evidence against it.
If incorrect: The columnist's conclusion is unbolded ('the belief is mistaken'); the first bold is the view being refuted.

Answer: A. The columnist's conclusion is 'the belief is mistaken.' The first bold portion is that popular belief (opposed); the second is evidence against it.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Scientist: <b>This drug does not reduce recovery time.</b> Its supporters cite a trial in which patients on the drug recovered faster, but <b>those patients were also much younger than the comparison group.</b> In the scientist's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is the scientist's conclusion; the second is evidence used to undermine a study offered against that conclusion.
  • BThe first is evidence; the second is the scientist's conclusion.
  • CBoth portions are conclusions the scientist draws.
  • DThe first is an objection; the second is the scientist's conclusion.
  • EThe first is the conclusion; the second is an assumption it relies on.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The first bold is a flat claim - likely the conclusion.
  2. The second bold weakens the opponents' trial - so it supports the scientist.
  3. Match: conclusion + supporting evidence.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - conclusion first, confounder-evidence second.
If incorrect: The 'younger patients' point backs the scientist by discrediting the rival trial.

Answer: A. The scientist concludes the drug doesn't help (first bold). The second bold points out a confounder (younger patients) that undercuts the supporting trial - it's evidence for the conclusion.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Official: <b>The city should build the new light-rail line.</b> Opponents cite its cost. Still, <b>the line is projected to remove thousands of cars from the roads each day, easing congestion.</b> In the official's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is the conclusion the official defends; the second is evidence offered to support it against an objection.
  • BThe first is an objection; the second is the conclusion.
  • CBoth portions are objections the official concedes.
  • DThe first is evidence; the second is the conclusion.
  • EThe first is the conclusion; the second is an assumption it requires.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. The official's conclusion is 'build the line' (first bold). The projected car reduction (second bold) is supporting evidence that answers the cost objection.

you_2 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Editor: Some argue that <b>printed newspapers will disappear within a decade.</b> Yet <b>subscriptions to several major print papers have grown steadily over the past five years.</b> The prediction is therefore doubtful. In the editor's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is a prediction the editor disputes; the second is evidence the editor uses against it.
  • BThe first is the editor's conclusion; the second supports it.
  • CBoth portions are the editor's evidence.
  • DThe first is evidence against the conclusion; the second is the conclusion.
  • EThe first is the editor's conclusion; the second is an objection to it.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. The editor concludes the prediction is doubtful. The first bold is that disputed prediction; the second is evidence (rising subscriptions) against it.

you_3 · you do (independent)hard

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Consultant: <b>The firm should adopt a four-day workweek.</b> Skeptics worry productivity will fall. However, <b>at similar firms that made the switch, output per employee stayed the same or rose.</b> In the consultant's argument, the two boldfaced portions play which of the following roles?
  • AThe first is the recommendation the consultant makes; the second is evidence offered to support it against a worry.
  • BThe first is a worry the consultant shares; the second is the recommendation.
  • CBoth portions are evidence for the recommendation.
  • DThe first is evidence; the second is the recommendation.
  • EThe first is the recommendation; the second is an objection to it.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. The consultant's recommendation (conclusion) is the four-day week (first bold). The comparable-firm data (second bold) supports it against the productivity worry.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)

If not yet mastered: Not yet: route every miss to the required error log (tag why: content gap / reasoning flaw / timing / careless / trap), schedule spaced re-practice via topic-aware scheduling, then re-enter the we-do -> you-do loop before re-testing.
