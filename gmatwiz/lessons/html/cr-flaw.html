Critical Reasoning: Find the Flaw

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::flaw · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify an argument's conclusion and evidence, recognize common reasoning errors · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Name the specific reasoning error an argument commits.
  • Recognize classic flaws: ad hominem, appeal to popularity, post hoc, hasty generalization, appeal to ignorance, slippery slope.
  • Match the abstract flaw description to what the argument actually did.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Separating an argument's conclusion from its evidence.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes.

  1. Name the flaw: 'X happened after Y, so Y caused X.'Post hoc (correlation/sequence mistaken for causation).
  2. Name the flaw: 'Most people buy it, so it's the best.'Appeal to popularity.
  3. Name the flaw: 'No one has proven it's harmful, so it's safe.'Appeal to ignorance.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls the named flaw families so learners can match them to the argument.
If students struggle: Have them describe in their own words what the author did wrong before reading the choices.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You can find a conclusion and its evidence. A flaw question asks WHY the leap between them is illegitimate - and the answer is usually one of a handful of named reasoning errors. Describe the mistake in your own words first, then match it.

Learning intention

By the end you can name the specific reasoning error an argument commits and match it to the correct abstract description.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can describe, in plain words, what the argument did wrong.
  • I can recognize the classic named flaws.
  • I can match my description to the answer choice that states it.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
Critic: The restaurant's owner says his food is the healthiest in town, but he is trying to sell meals, so his claim is worthless. The critic's reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Arejects a claim based on the source's motive rather than on any evidence about the claim itself.
  • Brelies on a sample of diners that is too small.
  • Cassumes that healthy food cannot be profitable.
  • Dconfuses a cause with an effect.
  • Etreats the owner's opinion as expert testimony.

Think-aloud

  1. Conclusion: the owner's claim is worthless. Evidence: he wants to sell meals.
  2. In my words: attacking the person's motive, not the claim - that's ad hominem.
  3. Find the choice describing that: A.
  4. The other choices name flaws the argument didn't commit.

Correct: A. The critic dismisses the health claim only because the owner has a motive to sell - an ad hominem that ignores whether the food is actually healthy. A names that error.

Takeaway. Describe the reasoning error in plain words first, then match it to the choice - don't be lured by real flaws the argument didn't make.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
Advertisement: More dentists choose BrightSmile toothpaste than any other brand, so BrightSmile must be the most effective toothpaste. The reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Atreats a product's popularity as proof of its effectiveness.
  • Brelies on the opinion of a single dentist.
  • Cassumes dentists never change brands.
  • Dignores the price of competing toothpastes.
  • Econfuses effectiveness with safety.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. What is the evidence, and what is concluded from it?
  2. Popularity -> quality is a classic named flaw.
  3. Match your description to the choice.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - popularity doesn't establish effectiveness.
If incorrect: The ad cites many dentists, not one; the core error is popularity -> quality.

Answer: A. The ad slides from 'most chosen' to 'most effective' - an appeal to popularity. A names that error.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Politician: No study has ever shown that our new tax policy harms small businesses, so the policy is clearly safe for them. The reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Atreats the absence of evidence of harm as proof that there is no harm.
  • Brelies on studies funded by the government.
  • Cassumes all small businesses are alike.
  • Dconfuses correlation with causation.
  • Eattacks the motives of the policy's critics.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The evidence is a LACK of evidence.
  2. Absence of proof of harm is not proof of safety.
  3. Which named flaw is that?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Exactly - appeal to ignorance.
If incorrect: The argument doesn't attack critics or cite studies; it leans on missing evidence.

Answer: A. Concluding the policy is safe merely because harm hasn't been shown is an appeal to ignorance. A names it.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Manager: Right after we repainted the office, output went up, so the new paint color is boosting productivity. The reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Aconcludes that the paint caused the rise simply because the rise came after the painting.
  • Brelies on the testimony of a few employees.
  • Cassumes all employees like the color.
  • Dignores the cost of the paint.
  • Etreats productivity and morale as the same thing.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Inferring causation from mere sequence (output rose after painting) is a post hoc flaw. A names it.

you_2 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Editorial: If the city allows food trucks on Main Street, soon every sidewalk will be blocked and no pedestrian will be able to walk downtown. So food trucks must be banned. The reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Aassumes without support that a limited step will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome.
  • Brelies on an unrepresentative survey.
  • Cattacks the character of food-truck owners.
  • Dconfuses a necessary condition with a sufficient one.
  • Etreats popularity as evidence of quality.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Claiming one modest allowance must snowball into total blockage is a slippery-slope flaw. A names it.

If you missed it, log it: content gapreasoning flawtimingcarelesstrap

you_3 · you do (independent)hard

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Reviewer: This author's last three novels were bestsellers, so her next book is certain to be a bestseller too. The reasoning is most vulnerable to the criticism that it
  • Aassumes that a past pattern must continue without considering factors that could change the outcome.
  • Brelies on the opinions of unqualified readers.
  • Cattacks the author rather than the books.
  • Dconfuses sales with literary quality.
  • Etreats the absence of failure as proof of success.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Projecting a past streak forward as a certainty ignores factors that could break the pattern - an unwarranted assumption of continuity. A names it.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
