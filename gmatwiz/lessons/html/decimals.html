Decimals

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::decimals · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs place value, fractions · ~20 min

Learning objectives

  • Multiply and divide decimals by tracking decimal places and shifting by powers of 10.
  • Convert fluently among fractions, decimals, and percents.
  • Round and estimate to sanity-check the size of an answer.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Place value and the fraction ↔ decimal link.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Write 0.75 as a fraction in lowest terms.3/4.
  2. Compute 0.2 × 0.3.0.06.
  3. Round 3.467 to the nearest hundredth.3.47.
  4. What fraction equals 0.125?1/8.
  5. Why is 0.2 × 0.3 = 0.06, not 0.6?Multiply the digits (2 × 3 = 6), then place the point by the TOTAL decimal places (1 + 1 = 2).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves decimal–fraction conversions and the decimal-place rule for multiplication — the foundation for the division and size-estimation problems coming up.
If students struggle: If place counting is shaky, model 0.2 × 0.3 as (2/10)(3/10) = 6/100 = 0.06 to anchor the rule in fractions.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Q5 explains the rule you'll lean on all lesson: a decimal is just a fraction over a power of ten. Once you see 0.2 = 2/10, multiplying and dividing decimals becomes counting places and shifting by powers of 10 — and converting to fractions is often the fastest GMAT move.

Learning intention

By the end you can multiply and divide decimals reliably, convert among fractions/decimals/percents, and estimate to check an answer's size.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can divide by a decimal by shifting both numbers to make the divisor whole.
  • I can convert a decimal to a reduced fraction and back.
  • I can predict roughly how big an answer should be before computing.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
Which of the following is equal to 0.0036 ÷ 0.04?
  • A0.9
  • B0.09
  • C0.009
  • D9
  • E0.0009

Think-aloud

  1. Dividing by a decimal is awkward; make the divisor a whole number.
  2. Multiply BOTH by 100: 0.0036 → 0.36 and 0.04 → 4.
  3. 0.36 ÷ 4 = 0.09.
  4. Size check: dividing by the small number 0.04 should give a small result — 0.09 fits.

Correct: B. Multiply both numbers by 100 to make the divisor whole: 0.0036 ÷ 0.04 = 0.36 ÷ 4 = 0.09.

Takeaway. To divide by a decimal, shift the decimal point in BOTH numbers until the divisor is a whole number.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
Compute 1.2 × 0.05.
  • A0.6
  • B0.06
  • C0.006
  • D6.0
  • E0.0006
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Drop the decimals: 12 × 5 = 60.
  2. Count decimal places in the factors: 1 (in 1.2) + 2 (in 0.05) = 3.
  3. Place the point 3 from the right: 0.060 = 0.06.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — 60 with three decimal places is 0.06.
If incorrect: Count places in BOTH factors (1 + 2 = 3), not just one.

Answer: B. Ignore the points: 12 × 5 = 60. Total decimal places = 1 + 2 = 3, so 0.060 = 0.06.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Write 7/8 as a decimal.
  • A0.78
  • B0.875
  • C0.825
  • D0.88
  • E0.7875
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Recall 1/8 = 0.125.
  2. 7/8 = 7 × (1/8).
  3. 7 × 0.125 = 0.875.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 0.875.
If incorrect: Use the benchmark 1/8 = 0.125 and multiply by 7, or long-divide 7 ÷ 8.

Answer: B. 1/8 = 0.125, so 7/8 = 7 × 0.125 = 0.875 (or divide 7 ÷ 8 = 0.875).
