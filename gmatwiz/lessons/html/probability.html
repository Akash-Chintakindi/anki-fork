Probability

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::probability · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs fractions, counting & combinatorics · ~24 min

Learning objectives

  • Compute basic probability as favorable ÷ total equally likely outcomes.
  • Use the complement rule P(not A) = 1 − P(A), especially for 'at least one'.
  • Combine events: 'and' (multiply, independent) vs 'or' (add, then subtract overlap).
  • Handle without-replacement draws by updating counts.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Fractions and counting outcomes.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. A fair die is rolled. P(rolling a 4)?1/6.
  2. A fair coin is flipped. P(heads)?1/2.
  3. A fair die is rolled. P(even number)?3/6 = 1/2.
  4. If P(rain) = 0.3, what is P(no rain)?0.7 (the complement).
  5. Two coin flips: why is P(two heads) = 1/4, not 1/2?The flips are independent, so multiply: 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves favorable-over-total, the complement, and the multiply-for-'and' rule — the three moves behind every problem today.
If students struggle: If the multiply rule is unclear, list all four outcomes of two flips (HH, HT, TH, TT) to show P(HH) = 1/4.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Probability is just counting wrapped in a fraction: favorable outcomes over total outcomes (your counting skills). Q4 and Q5 add the two power tools — the complement (turn 'at least one' into '1 minus none') and multiplying for 'and'. Today you'll combine them, including draws without replacement where the counts change.

Learning intention

By the end you can compute single-event probabilities, use the complement for 'at least one', and combine events with the 'and' (multiply) and 'or' (add minus overlap) rules.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can set up favorable ÷ total for an equally likely sample space.
  • I can solve 'at least one' problems via the complement.
  • I can chain dependent draws by updating the counts.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A bag contains 4 red and 6 green marbles. Two marbles are drawn at random without replacement. What is the probability that both are red?
  • A2/15
  • B4/25
  • C1/6
  • D2/5
  • E6/25

Think-aloud

  1. First draw: 4 red out of 10 → 4/10 = 2/5.
  2. No replacement: now 3 red remain out of 9 → 3/9 = 1/3.
  3. 'Both' means multiply: 2/5 × 1/3 = 2/15.

Correct: A. P(both red) = (4/10)(3/9) = (2/5)(1/3) = 2/15. Without replacement, the second draw uses the reduced counts.

Takeaway. 'And' (both happen) → multiply; without replacement, update the totals for the next draw.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
A fair die is rolled once. What is the probability of rolling a number greater than 4?
  • A1/6
  • B1/3
  • C1/2
  • D2/3
  • E1/4
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. List the outcomes greater than 4: 5 and 6.
  2. Total equally likely outcomes = 6.
  3. 2/6 reduces to 1/3.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — 1/3.
If incorrect: 'Greater than 4' excludes 4 itself; only 5 and 6 qualify.

Answer: B. Favorable outcomes are 5 and 6 → 2 out of 6 = 1/3.

we_2 · we do (guided)easy
The probability that an event occurs is 3/7. What is the probability it does NOT occur?
  • A3/7
  • B4/7
  • C3/4
  • D7/10
  • E1/7
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. P(not A) = 1 − P(A).
  2. 1 = 7/7.
  3. 7/7 − 3/7 = 4/7.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 4/7.
If incorrect: Subtract the probability from 1 (written as 7/7).

Answer: B. Complement: 1 − 3/7 = 4/7.

4You do — independent application

you_1 · you do (independent) · target 60smedium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A jar has 2 white, 3 black, and 5 red marbles. One marble is drawn at random. What is the probability it is NOT red?
  • A1/2
  • B1/5
  • C3/10
  • D2/5
  • E7/10
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Not red = (2 + 3)/10 = 5/10 = 1/2 (equivalently 1 − 5/10).

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Two fair coins are flipped. What is the probability of getting at least one head?
  • A1/4
  • B1/2
  • C2/3
  • D3/4
  • E1
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: D. Use the complement: P(no heads) = (1/2)(1/2) = 1/4, so P(at least one head) = 1 − 1/4 = 3/4.
