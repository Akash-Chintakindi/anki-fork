Descriptive Statistics

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::statistics · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs addition & division (for averages), ordering numbers · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Compute mean, median, mode, and range.
  • Use sum = mean × count to handle 'add/remove a value' and 'missing value' problems.
  • Reason about standard deviation as spread about the mean (no formula needed on the GMAT).
  • Compare measures of center for skewed data.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Addition, division, and putting numbers in order.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Find the mean of 4, 8, 9, 11.8 (sum 32 ÷ 4).
  2. Find the median of 3, 7, 9, 12, 20.9 (middle value).
  3. Find the mode of 2, 3, 3, 5, 7.3 (most frequent).
  4. Find the range of 5, 9, 14, 21.16 (21 − 5).
  5. If you add a value equal to the current mean, does the mean change?No — adding the mean itself leaves the average unchanged.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves the four basic measures and the key intuition (the mean is a balance point) that powers today's sum-based reasoning.
If students struggle: If median is shaky with an even count, remind them to sort first and average the two middle values.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You computed means by adding then dividing. The GMAT rarely asks that directly — it gives you the average and asks you to work backward. The move is to flip the formula into sum = mean × count. Q5's insight (the mean is a balance point) is why that flip works, and it's the key to every 'missing value' and 'new average' question today.

Learning intention

By the end you can compute mean/median/mode/range and use sum = mean × count to solve missing-value and changing-average problems, and reason qualitatively about standard deviation.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can convert between an average and a total using count.
  • I can find a missing value given an average.
  • I can say which of two data sets has the larger spread and why.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
The average (arithmetic mean) of 5 numbers is 12. If a sixth number, 30, is added to the set, what is the new average?
  • A13
  • B14
  • C15
  • D16
  • E18

Think-aloud

  1. Convert the average to a sum: 12 × 5 = 60.
  2. Add the new value: 60 + 30 = 90.
  3. Divide by the new count: 90 ÷ 6 = 15.

Correct: C. Sum = mean × count = 12 × 5 = 60. New sum = 60 + 30 = 90 over 6 numbers, so the new average = 90/6 = 15.

Takeaway. Don't average the averages — turn the mean into a SUM (mean × count), adjust, then divide by the new count.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
Find the median of 4, 1, 7, 3, 9, 6.
  • A4
  • B5
  • C6
  • D6.5
  • E7
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Always sort before finding a median.
  2. Six values → no single middle; use the two middle ones.
  3. Average the 3rd and 4th: (4 + 6)/2.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — 5.
If incorrect: Sort first, then average the two middle values for an even-sized list.

Answer: B. Sort: 1, 3, 4, 6, 7, 9. With 6 values the median is the average of the 3rd and 4th: (4 + 6)/2 = 5.

we_2 · we do (guided)easy
The mean of 6 numbers is 10. What is their sum?
  • A10
  • B16
  • C36
  • D60
  • E600
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Mean = sum ÷ count.
  2. Rearrange: sum = mean × count.
  3. 10 × 6.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 60.
If incorrect: Multiply the mean by how many numbers there are.

Answer: D. Sum = mean × count = 10 × 6 = 60.
