Critical Reasoning: Strengthen

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::strengthen · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify an argument's conclusion, spot the assumption connecting evidence to conclusion · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Find the conclusion, then choose the fact that makes it more likely to be true.
  • Prefer answers that confirm the argument's assumption or rule out an alternative cause.
  • Reject choices that are merely on-topic but do not affect the conclusion.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Finding an argument's assumption.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes, from memory.

  1. To STRENGTHEN a causal claim, what is one powerful move?Rule out an alternative cause, or show the cause-effect holds in a controlled comparison.
  2. Does a choice that repeats the conclusion strengthen it?No - restating is not new support.
  3. Strengthen or not: evidence that a confounding factor was absent.Strengthens (removes an alternative explanation).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that strengthening usually means confirming the assumption or eliminating a rival cause.
If students struggle: Remind them a strengthener need not prove the conclusion - just make it more likely.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You can find the assumption that props up an argument. A strengthen question rewards the choice that makes that assumption more secure - or slams the door on a competing explanation. Same gap, now you reinforce it instead of just naming it.

Learning intention

By the end you can pick the choice that most increases the likelihood the conclusion is true, usually by supporting the assumption or removing an alternative cause.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can state the conclusion the choice must support.
  • I can tell a real strengthener from an on-topic distractor.
  • I can recognize 'rule out another cause' as a strengthening move.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A town noticed that crime fell in the year after it installed brighter streetlights, and concluded the lights caused the drop. Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the conclusion?
  • AIn a neighboring town that installed no new lights that year, crime stayed the same.
  • BThe brighter streetlights were more energy-efficient than the old ones.
  • CResidents said they felt safer after the lights were installed.
  • DThe town had wanted brighter lighting for several years.
  • ECrime is generally higher in areas with more foot traffic.

Think-aloud

  1. Conclusion: the lights caused the crime drop (a causal claim).
  2. To strengthen a cause, rule out that something else caused the drop.
  3. A shows a comparable town without new lights had no drop - so the drop wasn't a general trend.
  4. That isolates the lights as the cause; the others don't touch causation.

Correct: A. A controlled comparison - a similar town with no new lights and no crime drop - rules out a region-wide trend, isolating the lights as the cause and strengthening the conclusion.

Takeaway. For causal conclusions, the strongest strengthener usually eliminates an alternative cause via a controlled comparison.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A company found that employees who use its new project-management software finish projects faster, and concluded the software makes teams more efficient. Which of the following, if true, most strengthens this conclusion?
  • AWhen a team that had been slow was switched to the software, its completion times improved.
  • BThe software was developed by an award-winning design firm.
  • CEmployees say the software is easy to learn.
  • DThe software is used by many large companies.
  • EFaster teams tend to have higher morale.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The claim is causal: software -> efficiency.
  2. Watch for reverse causation: maybe efficient teams adopt the software.
  3. Which choice shows the software CHANGING a team's speed?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - a before/after on the same team addresses reverse causation.
If incorrect: Popularity and design awards don't show the software caused the speed-up.

Answer: A. Showing the same previously-slow team sped up after adopting the software supports causation rather than the possibility that fast teams simply chose the software.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A nutrition writer argues that a new breakfast cereal improves focus, citing that children who eat it score higher on morning attention tests. Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?
  • ABefore eating the cereal, those same children had scored no higher than other children on the tests.
  • BThe cereal is fortified with several vitamins.
  • CThe cereal is popular among parents.
  • DAttention tests are administered by trained staff.
  • EChildren generally focus better after eating something.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Beware the alternative: maybe naturally focused kids eat this cereal.
  2. A before/after within the same children rules that out.
  3. Vitamins and popularity don't establish the focus effect.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - ruling out prior ability strengthens the causal link.
If incorrect: Choice E actually weakens by giving an alternative cause (any breakfast helps).

Answer: A. If the high scorers were ordinary before the cereal, the cereal - not pre-existing ability - is the likely cause of the improvement, strengthening the claim.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A retailer concluded that its new loyalty program increased customer spending, because members spend more than non-members. Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the conclusion?
  • ACustomers' spending rose after they joined the program, compared with their spending before joining.
  • BThe loyalty program was advertised on social media.
  • CMembers receive a discount on their birthdays.
  • DThe retailer has stores in many cities.
  • EHigh spenders enjoy collecting reward points.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. A within-customer before/after comparison shows the program changed behavior, rather than merely that big spenders join programs.

  • AUsers who were randomly assigned to the app reported lower stress than those assigned no app.

  • CThe app has millions of downloads.
  • DStress was measured with a standard questionnaire.

Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Random assignment rules out that already-calm people simply chose the app, giving strong support that the app itself lowers stress.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)

If not yet mastered: Not yet: route every miss to the required error log (tag why: content gap / reasoning flaw / timing / careless / trap), schedule spaced re-practice via topic-aware scheduling, then re-enter the we-do -> you-do loop before re-testing.
