Critical Reasoning: Complete the Argument

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::complete_argument · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify what an argument is trying to conclude, recognize a supporting vs. undermining statement · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Read the sentence just before the blank to learn what the blank must do.
  • Follow the connector: 'because/since' needs support; 'but/however' needs a contrast.
  • Choose the option that logically completes the intended direction of the argument.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Recognizing whether a statement supports or undermines a claim.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes.

  1. After 'because ___,' what must the blank provide?A reason that supports the preceding claim.
  2. After 'the plan will fail, since ___,' what must the blank do?Give a reason the plan will fail.
  3. Does the connector before the blank set the direction?Yes - 'because/since' = support; 'but/yet' = contrast.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that the word before the blank fixes what the completion must accomplish.
If students struggle: Have them read only the clause containing the blank and predict its job before scanning choices.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Complete-the-argument items are strengthen/weaken in disguise: the connector before the blank ('because,' 'since,' 'but') tells you whether the missing piece must support or push back, and you pick the option that does exactly that.

Learning intention

By the end you can use the connector before a blank to choose the option that logically completes the argument in the intended direction.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can identify the claim the blank must support or oppose.
  • I can use 'because/since' vs 'but/however' to set the direction.
  • I can pick the option that fills the blank in that direction.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
The town plans to reduce car accidents at a dangerous intersection by adding a traffic light. The plan is likely to succeed, because ___. Which of the following most logically completes the argument?
  • Amost accidents at that intersection are caused by drivers who are unsure when it is their turn to proceed
  • Bthe intersection is one of the busiest in the town
  • Ctraffic lights are expensive to install and maintain
  • Dthe town has added traffic lights at other intersections before
  • Emany drivers in the town exceed the speed limit

Think-aloud

  1. Conclusion: the traffic-light plan is likely to succeed.
  2. The connector is 'because,' so the blank must SUPPORT that.
  3. A light resolves right-of-way confusion; if that's the cause, the light works.
  4. A supports the plan; the others are neutral or don't explain success.

Correct: A. 'Because' means the blank must give a reason the light will succeed. If accidents stem from confusion over the right of way, a light - which assigns turns - directly fixes that cause. A completes the support.

Takeaway. Let the connector before the blank set the direction: 'because/since' needs support, 'but/however' needs a contrast.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A company believes that offering free coffee will make employees more productive. This belief may be mistaken, however, because ___. Which of the following most logically completes the argument?
  • Athe main thing slowing employees down is unclear project priorities, which free coffee does nothing to address
  • Bcoffee is inexpensive to provide in bulk
  • Cmany employees already drink coffee at home
  • Dthe office has space for a coffee machine
  • Esome employees prefer tea to coffee
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The connector 'however' plus 'because' means: give a reason the plan won't work.
  2. What would make free coffee fail to raise productivity?
  3. Pick the choice that names the real obstacle.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - if priorities are the bottleneck, coffee is beside the point.
If incorrect: Cost and preferences don't explain why productivity wouldn't rise.

Answer: A. 'However ... because' signals the blank must give a reason the belief is mistaken. If the real drag is unclear priorities, free coffee won't help - completing the doubt.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A bookstore expects that hosting weekly author events will increase book sales. This expectation is probably well founded, because ___. Which of the following most logically completes the argument?
  • Aat bookstores that host such events, attendees frequently buy the featured author's books on the spot
  • Bauthor events require setting up chairs and a microphone
  • Cthe bookstore has hosted a few events in the past
  • Dthe bookstore is open seven days a week
  • Esome authors are better public speakers than others
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The connector is 'because' - the blank must support the expectation.
  2. What fact would show events actually drive sales?
  3. Choose the supporting evidence, not logistics.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - on-the-spot buying supports the sales expectation.
If incorrect: Setup logistics and hours don't show sales will rise.

Answer: A. 'Because' requires support for the expectation. Evidence that attendees buy books at such events directly supports that the events raise sales.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A gym plans to attract more members by opening a swimming pool. The plan is unlikely to work, however, because ___. Which of the following most logically completes the argument?
  • Avery few people in the area have expressed any interest in swimming, and a large public pool already operates nearby
  • Bswimming is good low-impact exercise
  • Cthe pool will require regular cleaning
  • Dthe gym already has a large parking lot
  • Ethe gym is busiest in the evenings
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. 'However ... because' needs a reason the plan will fail. Low local interest plus an existing nearby pool means the new pool won't draw members - completing the doubt.

Answer: A. 'However ... because' needs a reason the belief may fail. If higher failure rates drive up warranty costs beyond the savings, the margin may not improve - completing the doubt.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)

If not yet mastered: Not yet: route every miss to the required error log (tag why: content gap / reasoning flaw / timing / careless / trap), schedule spaced re-practice via topic-aware scheduling, then re-enter the we-do -> you-do loop before re-testing.
