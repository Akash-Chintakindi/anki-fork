Data Insights: Data Sufficiency

Topic id gmat::di::reasoning::data_sufficiency · Domain Data Insights · Prereqs solve a simple linear equation for one unknown, recognize when given facts pin down a single value · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Evaluate each statement independently, then together, without computing the final answer.
  • Judge sufficiency by whether the value or yes/no answer is uniquely determined.
  • Map the result onto the correct one of the five standard Data Sufficiency choices.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Solving a linear equation and telling when facts fix a single value.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. Answer from memory in 4 minutes.

  1. Solve for x: 2x + 6 = 20.x = 7.
  2. From only a + b = 10, can you find a unique value for a?No - many pairs work (3 and 7, 4 and 6, ...).
  3. If a + b = 10 and a - b = 2, is the pair (a, b) determined?Yes - a = 6, b = 4.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that a unique answer needs enough independent constraints - the core idea Data Sufficiency tests.
If students struggle: Have them list two different number pairs that fit one equation to feel why a single fact is often not enough.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You already sense when facts pin down one answer and when they leave many options. Data Sufficiency makes that the whole task: you never compute the final answer - you decide whether the information is enough to determine it. The discipline is to test statement (1) alone, then statement (2) alone (forgetting (1)), then both together.

Learning intention

By the end you can decide, for a two-statement Data Sufficiency question, whether statement (1) alone, statement (2) alone, both together, or neither is sufficient - and choose the matching answer.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I evaluate each statement on its own before combining them.
  • I decide sufficiency by whether the answer is uniquely determined, not by computing it.
  • I map my finding onto the correct one of the five standard Data Sufficiency choices.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A bakery sold only muffins and scones one morning. How many muffins did it sell? (1) The bakery sold 40 baked goods in total that morning. (2) The bakery sold three times as many muffins as scones.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.

Think-aloud

  1. The question asks for a single number - how many muffins. In DS I judge sufficiency; I do not need the final count.
  2. Statement (1): total = 40, so muffins + scones = 40. Many splits fit (30 and 10, or 20 and 20). Not sufficient.
  3. Statement (2): muffins = 3 x scones. With no total, muffins could be 3, 30, or 300. Not sufficient.
  4. Together: muffins + scones = 40 and muffins = 3 x scones give 4 x scones = 40, so scones = 10 and muffins = 30 - unique. Sufficient.
  5. Neither alone works but both together do, so the answer is C.

Correct: C. We need one number: the muffin count. (1) muffins + scones = 40 allows many splits (30 and 10, 20 and 20, ...), not sufficient. (2) muffins = 3 x scones has no total, so muffins could be 3, 30, 300, not sufficient. Together: muffins + scones = 40 and muffins = 3 x scones give 4 x scones = 40, so scones = 10 and muffins = 30 - a unique value. Neither alone works but both together do, so the answer is C.

Takeaway. Data Sufficiency asks whether the information pins down a single answer. Test each statement alone first, then together, and pick the matching choice - without ever needing the final number.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A rectangular garden has a length of 12 meters. What is the area of the garden, in square meters? (1) The width of the garden is 5 meters. (2) The perimeter of the garden is greater than 30 meters.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The length (12 m) is already given, so you only need the width to get the area.
  2. Statement (1) gives the width outright - is the area then fixed?
  3. Statement (2) only bounds the width (perimeter > 30). Does a range of widths give one area?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - (1) fixes the width and thus the area; (2) gives only a range, so it cannot.
If incorrect: Remember the length is given. (1) alone nails the area; (2) leaves many possible widths.

Answer: A. Length = 12 is given, so only the width is missing. (1) width = 5 gives area = 12 x 5 = 60 - sufficient. (2) perimeter > 30 means 2(12 + w) > 30, so w > 3; width and thus area are not fixed - not sufficient. Statement (1) alone is sufficient; (2) alone is not, so the answer is A.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Is the positive integer n even? (1) n is a multiple of 3. (2) n is a multiple of 4.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Rephrase the goal: you need a yes/no answer that is always the same.
  2. Statement (1): multiples of 3 include 6 (even) and 9 (odd). Does that settle it?
  3. Statement (2): every multiple of 4 is 4 x (integer). Is that always even?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Exactly - every multiple of 4 is even, so (2) settles it; (1) allows both even and odd.
If incorrect: Test small cases: 3, 6, 9 for (1); 4, 8, 12 for (2). One statement always answers, the other does not.

Answer: B. This is a yes/no question. (1) a multiple of 3 could be 6 (even) or 9 (odd) - not a definite answer, not sufficient. (2) a multiple of 4 equals 4k, which is always even - a definite 'yes', sufficient. Statement (2) alone is sufficient, so the answer is B.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)easy

Application-first — attempt before revealing

What is the value of x? (1) 3x - 5 = 16. (2) x^2 = 49 and x > 0.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: D. (1) 3x - 5 = 16 gives 3x = 21, so x = 7 - a unique value, sufficient. (2) x^2 = 49 with x > 0 gives x = 7 (the negative root is excluded) - sufficient. Each statement alone determines x, so the answer is D.

you_2 · you do (independent)hard

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A jar contains only red, blue, and green marbles. How many green marbles are in the jar? (1) The jar contains 12 red marbles. (2) The jar contains twice as many blue marbles as red marbles.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: E. We need the green count. (1) red = 12 says nothing about green - not sufficient. (2) blue = 2 x red gives no green count (and no numbers on its own) - not sufficient. Together we know red = 12 and blue = 24, but with no total for the jar, green is still unknown - not sufficient. So the answer is E.

you_3 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Is quadrilateral Q a square? (1) Q has four sides of equal length. (2) Q has four right angles.
  • AStatement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
  • BStatement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
  • CBOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • DEACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
  • EStatements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: C. (1) four equal sides makes a rhombus, which may or may not be a square - not sufficient. (2) four right angles makes a rectangle, which may or may not be a square - not sufficient. Together, four equal sides and four right angles force a square - sufficient. So the answer is C.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)
