Counting & Combinatorics

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::counting · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs multiplication principle, factorials · ~24 min

Learning objectives

  • Apply the fundamental counting principle (multiply independent choices).
  • Decide whether order matters: permutation vs combination.
  • Compute permutations P(n,k) and combinations C(n,k) without a calculator.
  • Handle simple restrictions (no repeats, fixed roles, by-category choices).

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Multiplication and the idea of independent choices.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. How many 2-digit codes use digits 1–4 if repeats are allowed?16 (4 × 4).
  2. Compute 4! (4 factorial).24.
  3. How many ways to arrange A, B, C in a row?6 (3!).
  4. Pick 1 of 3 shirts and 1 of 2 pants — how many outfits?6 (3 × 2).
  5. Choosing a 2-person team from a group — does order matter? Why?No — {A,B} is the same team as {B,A}; that's a combination.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves the multiplication principle, factorials, and the order-vs-no-order distinction that decides permutation versus combination.
If students struggle: If factorials are shaky, write 4! = 4·3·2·1 explicitly and connect to arranging 4 items.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Everything in counting grows from the multiplication principle you just used for outfits. Q5 splits the world in two: when order matters you keep all arrangements (permutations); when it doesn't, you divide out the repeats (combinations). Today you'll pick the right tool and compute it by hand.

Learning intention

By the end you can count outcomes with the multiplication principle and decide between permutations and combinations, computing each without a calculator.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can multiply independent choices to count outcomes.
  • I can tell whether a problem needs a permutation or a combination.
  • I can evaluate P(n,k) and C(n,k) by canceling factorials.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A committee of 3 people is chosen from a group of 7. How many different committees are possible?
  • A21
  • B35
  • C105
  • D210
  • E343

Think-aloud

  1. Does order matter? No — a committee {A,B,C} is the same in any order. Use combinations.
  2. C(7,3) = 7! / (3! · 4!) = (7·6·5)/(3·2·1).
  3. Numerator 7·6·5 = 210; denominator 3·2·1 = 6.
  4. 210 / 6 = 35.

Correct: B. A committee is unordered, so use a combination: C(7,3) = (7·6·5)/(3·2·1) = 210/6 = 35.

Takeaway. Order matters → permutation; order doesn't → combination. Committees, teams, and groups are combinations.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
In how many ways can 5 people line up in a row?
  • A20
  • B25
  • C60
  • D120
  • E720
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. A row is ordered → permutation.
  2. All five are arranged → 5!.
  3. 5·4·3·2·1.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — 120.
If incorrect: Arranging all n distinct items in order is n!, not a combination.

Answer: D. A line is an ordered arrangement of all 5: 5! = 5·4·3·2·1 = 120.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
From 6 books, in how many ways can you choose 2 to take on a trip?
  • A12
  • B15
  • C30
  • D36
  • E720
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Order doesn't matter for which 2 books → combination.
  2. C(6,2) = (6·5)/(2·1).
  3. 30/2 = 15.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 15.
If incorrect: 30 is the ordered count (P(6,2)); divide by 2! because the 2 books' order doesn't matter.

Answer: B. Choosing (order irrelevant) → combination: C(6,2) = (6·5)/(2·1) = 15.
