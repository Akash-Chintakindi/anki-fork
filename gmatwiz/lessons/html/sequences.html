Sequences

Topic id gmat::quant::algebra::sequences · Domain Algebra · Prereqs functions, algebraic expressions · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Identify arithmetic (constant difference) vs geometric (constant ratio) sequences.
  • Use the arithmetic nth-term formula a_n = a_1 + (n − 1)d.
  • Use the geometric nth-term formula a_n = a_1 · r^(n−1).
  • Find the sum of an arithmetic sequence with (n/2)(first + last).

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Function evaluation and recognizing patterns.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Next term: 3, 7, 11, 15, ___ .19 (add 4).
  2. Next term: 2, 6, 18, 54, ___ .162 (multiply by 3).
  3. Common difference of 5, 8, 11, 14?3.
  4. Common ratio of 1, 2, 4, 8?2.
  5. Why is a formula faster than listing to find the 100th term of 3, 7, 11, …?A formula jumps straight to term 100 instead of writing 99 terms first.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves the difference/ratio distinction and the need for a direct formula — exactly what the nth-term rules provide today.
If students struggle: If difference vs ratio blurs, ask 'add or multiply to advance?' — add = arithmetic, multiply = geometric.

Prior-knowledge bridge

A sequence is a function whose input is the term number n. You spotted the step rule in the Do-Now; today you turn that rule into a formula so you can leap to the 20th or 100th term without listing. Arithmetic adds a fixed d; geometric multiplies by a fixed r — same idea, different operation.

Learning intention

By the end you can find any term of an arithmetic or geometric sequence with its formula and sum an arithmetic sequence.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can apply a_n = a_1 + (n − 1)d for arithmetic sequences.
  • I can apply a_n = a_1 · r^(n−1) for geometric sequences.
  • I can sum an arithmetic sequence with (n/2)(first + last).

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
In the arithmetic sequence 4, 7, 10, 13, …, what is the 20th term?
  • A58
  • B60
  • C61
  • D64
  • E67

Think-aloud

  1. First term a_1 = 4; common difference d = 7 − 4 = 3.
  2. Use a_n = a_1 + (n − 1)d.
  3. a_20 = 4 + (20 − 1)(3) = 4 + 57 = 61.

Correct: C. a_1 = 4, d = 3. a_20 = a_1 + (n − 1)d = 4 + 19·3 = 4 + 57 = 61.

Takeaway. Arithmetic nth term: a_n = a_1 + (n − 1)d. The jump count is (n − 1), not n.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
Find the 10th term of the arithmetic sequence with a_1 = 5 and d = 4.
  • A36
  • B40
  • C41
  • D45
  • E49
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Use a_n = a_1 + (n − 1)d.
  2. n = 10 means 9 jumps of size 4.
  3. 5 + 9(4) = 41.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — 41.
If incorrect: Multiply d by (n − 1) = 9, not by 10.

Answer: C. a_10 = 5 + (10 − 1)(4) = 5 + 36 = 41.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
In the geometric sequence 3, 6, 12, 24, …, what is the next term after 24?
  • A36
  • B30
  • C48
  • D96
  • E27
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Find the ratio between consecutive terms: 6/3 = 2.
  2. Geometric sequences multiply by r each step.
  3. 24 × 2 = 48.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 48.
If incorrect: This sequence multiplies (×2), it doesn't add a constant.

Answer: C. Common ratio r = 6/3 = 2; next term = 24 × 2 = 48.
