Reading Comprehension: Function

Topic id gmat::verbal::rc::function · Domain Reading Comprehension · Prereqs identify a passage's main point, see how a sentence relates to that point · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Explain WHY the author includes a specific detail (its role), not just what it says.
  • Common roles: give an example, support a claim, raise an objection, provide contrast.
  • Reject options that merely restate the detail or misstate its role.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Seeing how a sentence supports or complicates a main point.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes.

  1. A function question asks what about a detail?Why the author included it - its role in the passage.
  2. Name two common roles a detail can play.Give an example; support (or object to) a claim.
  3. Is restating the detail a correct function answer?No - you must say what job it does.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that function = the author's reason for including a detail.
If students struggle: Ask 'what would be lost if this sentence were deleted?' to reveal its role.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You can see how a sentence relates to the main point. A function question makes that explicit: name the JOB a detail does - example, support, objection, contrast - rather than repeating what it says.

Learning intention

By the end you can state the role a specific detail plays in a passage and match it to the right option.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can say why the author included a detail, not just what it says.
  • I can name the role (example, support, objection, contrast).
  • I can reject options that restate the detail or misname its role.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
Passage: Some claim that working from home lowers productivity. Yet at one large firm, output per employee rose after a shift to remote work - evidence that the claim is not universally true. Question: The author mentions the large firm's rising output primarily in order to
  • Aprovide evidence against the claim that remote work lowers productivity
  • Bprove that all firms should adopt remote work
  • Cdescribe how the firm measures output
  • Dintroduce a new claim about employee morale
  • Eexplain why some people work from home

Think-aloud

  1. Main point: the 'remote lowers productivity' claim isn't universally true.
  2. The firm's rising output is the evidence for that point.
  3. So its role is to counter the claim - choice A.

Correct: A. The firm's rising output is offered right after the claim, as counter-evidence ('evidence that the claim is not universally true'). Its role is to rebut the claim. A names that role.

Takeaway. Name the JOB a detail does for the argument, not what it literally says.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
Passage: Vitamin supplements are widely believed to prevent colds. In a large trial, however, people taking the supplement caught colds just as often as those taking a placebo. Question: The author mentions the trial primarily in order to
  • Achallenge the belief that supplements prevent colds
  • Brecommend a specific brand of supplement
  • Cexplain how placebos are manufactured
  • Dprove that colds are impossible to prevent
  • Edescribe the symptoms of the common cold
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. What belief is stated first?
  2. The trial is introduced with 'however' - a contrast signal.
  3. So the trial's job is to counter the belief.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - the trial challenges the belief.
If incorrect: D is too strong; the trial challenges ONE belief, not all prevention.

Answer: A. The trial's result (no difference from placebo) is introduced with 'however' to push back on the belief. Its role is to challenge that belief. A matches.

we_2 · we do (guided)easy
Passage: The new policy has clear benefits. It will, for instance, cut commute times for thousands of workers who currently drive across town each day. Question: The reference to cutting commute times functions primarily to
  • Agive a specific example of the policy's benefits
  • Braise an objection to the policy
  • Cprove the policy has no drawbacks
  • Ddefine what a commute is
  • Ecompare the policy to an older one
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The signal 'for instance' tells you the role.
  2. It illustrates the 'clear benefits' claim.
  3. That's an example, not an objection.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - it's an illustrative example.
If incorrect: 'For instance' signals an example, not an objection or proof of no drawbacks.

Answer: A. 'For instance' marks the commute detail as an example illustrating the stated benefits. A names that role; the others misstate it.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

Passage: Critics say the museum's new wing clashes with the old building. Supporters counter that the contrast draws visitors in - attendance has risen 30 percent since the wing opened. Question: The author mentions the 30 percent rise in attendance primarily in order to
  • Asupport the supporters' response to the critics
  • Bconcede that the critics are correct
  • Cexplain how attendance is counted
  • Dargue that the old building should be demolished
  • Edescribe the design of the new wing
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. The attendance rise backs the supporters' counter to the critics. Its role is to support that response. A matches; the others misname it.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)

If not yet mastered: Not yet: route every miss to the required error log (tag why: content gap / reasoning flaw / timing / careless / trap), schedule spaced re-practice via topic-aware scheduling, then re-enter the we-do -> you-do loop before re-testing.
