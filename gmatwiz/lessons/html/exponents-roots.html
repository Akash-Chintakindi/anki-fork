Exponents & Roots

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::exponents_roots · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs number properties (prime factorization), multiplication · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Apply the exponent rules: product, quotient, power-of-a-power, zero, and negative exponents.
  • Interpret roots as fractional exponents and simplify radicals.
  • Solve a^x = N by rewriting N as a power of a and matching exponents.
  • Compare powers by expressing them on a common base.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Repeated multiplication and prime factorization.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Compute 2^3 and 5^2.8 and 25.
  2. Simplify x^2 · x^3.x^5.
  3. What is √36?6.
  4. What is x^0 for x ≠ 0?1.
  5. Why is x^2 · x^3 = x^5, not x^6?Multiplying powers of the same base ADDS exponents — you're counting total factors (2 + 3 = 5).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves the product rule, square roots, and the zero exponent — the building blocks for power-of-a-power, fractional exponents, and same-base comparisons today.
If students struggle: If the product rule is fuzzy, expand it: x^2 · x^3 = (x·x)(x·x·x) = x^5. Counting factors makes 'add exponents' obvious.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Q5 is the engine of this whole topic: every exponent rule is just careful factor-counting, and prime factorization (last week) is how you put two different-looking powers on the same base. Today you'll extend 'add the exponents' to dividing, nesting, and fractional exponents (roots) — and use a common base to compare powers fast.

Learning intention

By the end you can simplify expressions with the exponent rules, convert between roots and fractional exponents, and solve and compare exponential expressions by matching bases.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can simplify products, quotients, and powers of powers without errors.
  • I can rewrite a root as a fractional exponent and evaluate it.
  • I can compare powers by putting them on a common base.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
If 2^x = 8 and 3^y = 81, what is x + y?
  • A5
  • B6
  • C7
  • D12
  • E24

Think-aloud

  1. 8 is a power of 2: 8 = 2^3, so x = 3.
  2. 81 is a power of 3: 81 = 3^4, so y = 4.
  3. x + y = 3 + 4 = 7.

Correct: C. Rewrite each side on its own base: 8 = 2^3 so x = 3; 81 = 3^4 so y = 4; x + y = 7.

Takeaway. To solve a^x = N, rewrite N as a power of a, then match the exponents.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
Simplify (x^3)^4 · x^2.
  • Ax^9
  • Bx^14
  • Cx^24
  • Dx^20
  • Ex^12
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Inner first: (x^3)^4 means multiply exponents → x^12.
  2. Now you have x^12 · x^2.
  3. Same base, multiplying → add exponents: 12 + 2.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — x^14.
If incorrect: Multiply for power-of-a-power, then ADD for the product. Don't mix the two.

Answer: B. Power of a power multiplies: (x^3)^4 = x^12. Then multiply like bases by adding: x^12 · x^2 = x^14.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
Simplify √72.
  • A6√2
  • B8√3
  • C36
  • D12
  • E9√2
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Find the largest perfect square dividing 72.
  2. 72 = 36 × 2 and 36 is a perfect square.
  3. √(36·2) = √36 · √2 = 6√2.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 6√2.
If incorrect: Factor out a perfect square (36), not just any factor, so the root comes out whole.

Answer: A. Pull out the largest perfect-square factor: 72 = 36 × 2, so √72 = √36 · √2 = 6√2.
