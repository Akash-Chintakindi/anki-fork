Critical Reasoning: Weaken

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::weaken · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify an argument's conclusion, spot the assumption connecting evidence to conclusion · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Find the conclusion, then choose the fact that makes it less likely to be true.
  • Attack the assumption, or supply an alternative cause for the evidence.
  • Ignore choices that are irrelevant or that merely fail to help the argument.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Finding an argument's assumption and how a strengthener supports it.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes, from memory.

  1. What is the most common way to weaken a causal claim?Provide an alternative cause for the observed effect.
  2. Does a choice that is merely irrelevant weaken an argument?No - it must make the conclusion less likely.
  3. Weaken or not: the two groups compared differed in another important way.Weakens (introduces a confounder).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that weakening usually means attacking the assumption or offering a rival cause.
If students struggle: Remind them the correct answer need not disprove the conclusion - just dent it.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Strengthening reinforced the assumption; weakening does the opposite - it pries the assumption loose or hands you a different explanation for the evidence. Find the gap, then widen it.

Learning intention

By the end you can pick the choice that most reduces the likelihood the conclusion is true, usually by attacking the assumption or providing an alternative cause.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can state the conclusion the choice must undercut.
  • I can tell a real weakener from an irrelevant choice.
  • I can spot 'here's another cause' as a weakening move.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A bakery introduced a rewards card and its sales rose the next month, so the owner concluded the rewards card increased sales. Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the conclusion?
  • AThe bakery's only nearby competitor closed for renovations that same month.
  • BThe rewards card was printed on recycled paper.
  • CSome customers forgot to bring their rewards card.
  • DThe bakery had offered a rewards card once before.
  • EThe bakery sells both bread and pastries.

Think-aloud

  1. Conclusion: the rewards card caused higher sales (causal).
  2. To weaken a cause, find another explanation for the effect.
  3. A competitor closing that month explains the rise without the card.
  4. The other choices are irrelevant to what caused the increase.

Correct: A. A competitor closing for the month offers an alternative cause for the sales bump, undermining the claim that the rewards card was responsible.

Takeaway. For causal conclusions, the strongest weakener usually supplies an alternative cause for the evidence.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A manager concluded that a new training program improved her team's sales, since sales rose after the training. Which of the following, if true, most weakens the conclusion?
  • AThe company launched a large price promotion the same week as the training.
  • BThe training program lasted two full days.
  • CSome team members had attended similar training before.
  • DThe trainer was hired from outside the company.
  • ESales are tracked by an automated system.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. The claim is causal: training -> higher sales.
  2. Look for something else that could have raised sales at the same time.
  3. Which choice offers a competing explanation?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - a concurrent promotion undercuts the causal claim.
If incorrect: Details about the trainer or tracking don't change what caused the rise.

Answer: A. A simultaneous price promotion is an alternative cause for the sales rise, so the training may not deserve the credit.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A health official argued that a public-awareness campaign reduced smoking, because smoking rates fell after the campaign. Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument?
  • AA steep national tax on cigarettes took effect at the same time as the campaign.
  • BThe campaign used billboards and radio ads.
  • CSome smokers said they never saw the campaign.
  • DThe campaign was funded by a health charity.
  • ESmoking rates are measured through annual surveys.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. What else, happening at the same time, is known to cut smoking?
  2. That competing cause weakens the campaign's claim.
  3. Ad formats and funding don't address causation.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - the tax is a strong alternative cause.
If incorrect: 'Some never saw it' is minor; a nationwide tax is a much bigger competing cause.

Answer: A. A simultaneous cigarette tax is a well-known driver of reduced smoking, giving an alternative cause for the decline and weakening the campaign's claimed effect.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A store owner concluded that moving the checkout counter increased impulse purchases, since such purchases rose after the move. Which of the following, if true, most weakens the conclusion?
  • AThe store began stocking popular candy and magazines near the checkout at the same time.
  • BThe new counter is made of a more durable material.
  • CThe move took place over a single weekend.
  • DThe store has two entrances.
  • EImpulse purchases are recorded at the register.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Adding tempting items by the checkout is an alternative cause for the rise in impulse buys, independent of the counter's location.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • >=85% correct on new/held-out-style items (not flashcard recall) per session
  • sustained across >=2 spaced sessions with a delay between them
  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
  • per-topic performance surfaced only after >=8 application attempts (PRD give-up rule)

If not yet mastered: Not yet: route every miss to the required error log (tag why: content gap / reasoning flaw / timing / careless / trap), schedule spaced re-practice via topic-aware scheduling, then re-enter the we-do -> you-do loop before re-testing.
