Number Properties

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::number_properties · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs whole-number multiplication & division facts, place value · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Apply even/odd and sign rules to predict the result of an expression.
  • Use divisibility rules (2, 3, 5, 6, 9, 10) and the idea that divisibility by a composite means divisibility by its prime parts.
  • Prime-factorize an integer and use it to count factors and find GCD/LCM.
  • Translate 'must be true' number-property statements into fast tests.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Whole-number multiplication/division facts and place value.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes, no calculator. Answer from memory in 4 minutes.

  1. List all positive factors of 24.1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
  2. Is 51 prime? Show why in one product.No: 51 = 3 × 17.
  3. Compute 7 × 8 and 9 × 6.56 and 54.
  4. Is 0 even or odd?Even (0 = 2 × 0).
  5. What must be true for a number to be divisible by 6?Divisible by 2 AND by 3.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Surfaces factors, primes, even/odd, and the key idea that divisibility by a composite equals divisibility by its prime parts — exactly the tools today extends to factor-counting and GCD/LCM.
If students struggle: If factors of 24 are shaky, rebuild with a factor-pair ladder (1×24, 2×12, 3×8, 4×6) before moving on; today's factor counting depends on it.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You just found the factors of 24 by trial. That works for small numbers, but the GMAT asks about numbers like 72 — or 2³ × 3² × 5 — where listing is far too slow. Today we replace listing with prime factorization, the 'DNA' of an integer, which lets you count factors and find GCD/LCM in seconds.

Learning intention

By the end you can prime-factorize an integer and use it to count its factors, find GCD/LCM, and settle 'must be true' even/odd and divisibility questions without listing.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can write any integer up to ~200 as a product of primes.
  • I can count an integer's positive factors from its prime factorization.
  • I can predict the even/odd and the sign of an expression and justify it.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
How many distinct positive factors does 72 have?
  • A6
  • B8
  • C10
  • D12
  • E14

Think-aloud

  1. Prime-factorize: 72 = 8 × 9 = 2³ × 3².
  2. Any factor uses 0–3 twos (4 choices) and 0–2 threes (3 choices).
  3. Independent choices multiply: 4 × 3 = 12 factors.
  4. Spot-check by listing: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 72 — twelve.

Correct: D. 72 = 2³ × 3². The number of positive factors is (3+1)(2+1) = 4 × 3 = 12.

Takeaway. Number of positive factors = product of (each prime's exponent + 1).

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
If a is odd and b is even, which of the following must be ODD?
  • Aa + b
  • Bab
  • Cb − 2
  • Da + b + 1
  • E2a
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Parity rules: even ± even = even; odd ± even = odd.
  2. Anything multiplied by an even number is even.
  3. Test with a = 3, b = 2 and check each option's parity.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — odd + even is always odd; the ×even and +even options are forced even.
If incorrect: Re-check parity: B and E have an even factor (even); C subtracts an even number (stays even); D adds 1 to an odd, making it even.

Answer: A. a is odd and b is even, so a + b = odd + even = odd. ab and 2a have an even factor (even); b − 2 is even − even = even; a + b + 1 flips a + b to even. Only a + b is odd.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
What is the greatest common divisor (GCD) of 48 and 60?
  • A4
  • B6
  • C12
  • D16
  • E24
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Prime-factorize both numbers first.
  2. GCD uses only the primes they share, each at its smaller exponent.
  3. Shared primes: 2 (lowest power 2²) and 3 (lowest power 3¹).
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 2² × 3 = 12 divides both, and nothing larger does.
If incorrect: Don't multiply every prime. 5 appears only in 60, so it can't be in the GCD; use shared primes at their lowest exponent.

Answer: C. 48 = 2⁴ × 3 and 60 = 2² × 3 × 5. The GCD takes each SHARED prime at its LOWEST power: 2² × 3 = 12.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent) · target 75smedium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

If the product of two integers is odd, which of the following MUST be true?
  • ABoth integers are odd
  • BBoth integers are even
  • CExactly one integer is even
  • DTheir product is divisible by 2
  • EAt least one integer is even
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. A product is odd only when every factor is odd; a single even factor would make the product even. So both integers must be odd. Every other option requires or implies an even factor.

you_4 · you do (independent) · target 90shard

Application-first — attempt before revealing

If p and q are distinct prime numbers, each greater than 2, which of the following MUST be even?
  • Ap + q
  • Bpq
  • C2p + 1
  • Dpq + 2
  • Ep + q + 1
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Primes greater than 2 are odd. Odd + odd = even, so p + q is even. pq is odd; pq + 2 stays odd; 2p + 1 is odd; p + q + 1 is even + 1 = odd. Only p + q must be even.

What is the least common multiple (LCM) of 9 and 12?
  • A18
  • B24
  • C36
