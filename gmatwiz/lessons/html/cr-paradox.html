Critical Reasoning: Explain the Paradox

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::explain_paradox · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs state two facts that seem to conflict, distinguish an explanation from a mere comment · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Pin down the two facts that seem to contradict each other.
  • Choose the option that lets BOTH facts be true at once.
  • Reject options that deepen the mystery or address only one fact.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Reading two facts and noticing the tension between them.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. 4 minutes.

  1. What does a paradox/resolve question ask you to do?Explain how two seemingly conflicting facts can both be true.
  2. Should the right answer make one fact false?No - both facts stay true; the answer explains the gap.
  3. Resolve: prices rose but revenue fell.So many customers left that the loss outweighed the higher price.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls that a resolution must let both surprising facts coexist.
If students struggle: Have them write the two facts as 'X, yet Y' and look for the missing bridge.

Prior-knowledge bridge

A paradox question hands you two facts that seem to clash. You're not judging an argument - you're finding the missing piece of information that makes the clash disappear so both facts can be true together.

Learning intention

By the end you can identify two conflicting facts and pick the option that reconciles them without denying either.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can restate the paradox as 'X, yet surprisingly Y.'
  • I can pick the option that lets both X and Y be true.
  • I can reject options that ignore one fact or make it worse.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A coffee shop replaced its paper cups with more expensive reusable-style cups, yet its total cup costs for the year went down. Which of the following, if true, best explains this result?
  • ACustomers who brought the reusable-style cups back for refills sharply reduced the number of new cups the shop had to buy.
  • BThe reusable-style cups come in several colors.
  • CThe shop's coffee sales rose slightly during the year.
  • DPaper cups had been the shop's cheapest supply.
  • EThe new cups are made by a local manufacturer.

Think-aloud

  1. Fact 1: each new cup is more expensive. Fact 2: total cup costs fell (surprising).
  2. I need a reason total cost could drop despite higher unit price.
  3. A: reuse cut the NUMBER of cups bought - fewer cups times higher price can still be less.
  4. Both facts remain true; the other choices don't explain the drop.

Correct: A. Both facts stay true: the cups cost more each, yet total cup costs fell because far fewer cups were purchased overall. A supplies the bridge; the others don't reconcile the two facts.

Takeaway. A good resolution keeps both surprising facts true and supplies the missing information that removes the conflict.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A factory installed faster machines expecting to produce more units per day, but its daily output actually fell. Which of the following, if true, best explains the drop?
  • AThe faster machines broke down often and sat idle awaiting repairs for long stretches.
  • BThe faster machines use more electricity.
  • CThe factory kept the same number of workers.
  • DThe old machines had been in use for years.
  • EThe new machines are larger than the old ones.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. State it as 'faster machines, yet lower output.'
  2. What could make faster machines produce less overall?
  3. Look for downtime, not for irrelevant details.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - idle time from breakdowns explains the paradox.
If incorrect: Electricity use and size don't explain why fewer units came out.

Answer: A. Frequent breakdowns and idle time can lower output even though the machines are faster when running - reconciling 'faster machines' with 'lower output.'

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A library extended its hours to increase the number of visitors, but the number of visitors decreased afterward. Which of the following, if true, best explains this outcome?
  • ATo fund the longer hours, the library cut its budget for new books, and many regulars stopped coming once new titles grew scarce.
  • BThe library is located near a university.
  • CThe extended hours were advertised on the library's website.
  • DThe library employs several part-time staff.
  • ELibrary visits are counted by an electronic gate.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Frame it: 'longer hours, yet fewer visitors.'
  2. What trade-off could the longer hours have caused?
  3. The bridge must explain why visitors fell.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes - a hidden trade-off (fewer new books) explains the decline.
If incorrect: Location and staffing don't account for the drop in visitors.

Answer: A. The longer hours came at the cost of fewer new books, driving regulars away; both facts hold, and A bridges them.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A phone maker lowered the price of its flagship model, expecting to sell more units, but it sold fewer units than before. Which of the following, if true, best explains this result?
  • ABuyers took the lower price as a sign the model was about to be discontinued and avoided it.
  • BThe phone comes in three colors.
  • CThe price cut was announced online.
  • DThe company also makes tablets.
  • EThe phone's battery lasts a full day.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. If the lower price signaled impending discontinuation, buyers would shy away - explaining why a price cut reduced sales while both facts remain true.

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A city added dozens of new bus routes to reduce car traffic, yet car traffic increased in the following year. Which of the following, if true, best explains this?
  • AThe new routes made the city more attractive to newcomers, and the resulting population growth put more cars on the road than the buses removed.
  • BThe buses run on a fixed schedule.
  • CThe new routes were mapped by transit planners.
  • DBus fares stayed the same.
  • EThe buses are wheelchair-accessible.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Population growth spurred by better transit can add more cars than the buses take off the road, letting both facts be true.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2

  • per-topic memory surfaced only after >=20 graded reviews (PRD give-up rule)
