Percents

Topic id gmat::quant::arithmetic::percents · Domain Arithmetic · Prereqs fractions, decimals · ~22 min

Learning objectives

  • Convert among percents, fractions, and decimals.
  • Compute a percent of a number and find percent change (increase/decrease).
  • Handle successive (compound) percents by multiplying factors, not adding.
  • Reverse a percent: find the original from a part and its percent.

Model: I do → we do → you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Fractions and decimals — 'percent' means 'per hundred'.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

From memory, no calculator. 4 minutes.

  1. Write 25% as a fraction in lowest terms.1/4.
  2. What is 10% of 80?8.
  3. Convert 0.45 to a percent.45%.
  4. Write 1/5 as a percent.20%.
  5. If a price goes up 50% then down 50%, are you back to the start?No — the 50% drop is taken from the larger amount (100 → 150 → 75).

Retrieval starter (5 min). Retrieves conversions and 'percent of a number', and surfaces the #1 percent misconception (successive percents don't add) that today's lesson resolves.
If students struggle: If 10% of 80 is hard, anchor '10% = move the decimal one place'; build 5% and 15% from there.

Prior-knowledge bridge

Q5 is the trap the GMAT sets constantly: people add percents. You can't, because each percent acts on a different base. Today you'll treat every percent as a multiplier (15% off = ×0.85) and chain them — the same equivalent-fraction thinking you used with decimals, now applied to change.

Learning intention

By the end you can compute percent-of and percent-change, chain successive percents by multiplying, and reverse a percent to find the original amount.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can rewrite any percent as a multiplier (e.g., +20% = ×1.20).
  • I can compute percent change as change ÷ original.
  • I can find the original quantity when given a part and its percent.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A shirt priced at $80 is discounted 25%, then an additional 10% is taken off the reduced price. What is the final price?
  • A$52
  • B$54
  • C$56
  • D$58
  • E$60

Think-aloud

  1. 25% off means you pay 75%: 80 × 0.75 = 60.
  2. The next 10% comes off $60, not $80: 60 × 0.90 = 54.
  3. Final price is $54 — equivalent to ×0.675, i.e., 32.5% off, not 35%.

Correct: B. Apply discounts in sequence: 80 × 0.75 = 60, then 60 × 0.90 = 54.

Takeaway. Successive percents MULTIPLY (×0.75 then ×0.90 = ×0.675); they never simply add.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)easy
What is 15% of 240?
  • A24
  • B30
  • C36
  • D40
  • E48
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Find 10% first: move the decimal one place → 24.
  2. 5% is half of 10% → 12.
  3. 15% = 10% + 5% = 24 + 12.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right — building from 10% keeps it calculator-free.
If incorrect: Decompose: 15% = 10% + 5%. Find each, then add.

Answer: C. 10% of 240 = 24, and 5% = 12, so 15% = 24 + 12 = 36.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A quantity increases from 50 to 65. What is the percent increase?
  • A15%
  • B23%
  • C30%
  • D65%
  • E130%
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Percent change uses the ORIGINAL as the base.
  2. Change = 65 − 50 = 15.
  3. 15/50 = 0.30.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Yes — 30% increase.
If incorrect: Divide the change by the original (50), not by the new value (65).

Answer: C. Percent change = change ÷ original = (65 − 50)/50 = 15/50 = 0.30 = 30%.
