Critical Reasoning: Assumption

Topic id gmat::verbal::cr::assumption · Domain Critical Reasoning · Prereqs identify an argument's conclusion, separate premises from the conclusion · ~18 min

Learning objectives

  • Locate the conclusion and the evidence, then find the gap between them.
  • State the unstated premise the argument needs in order to hold.
  • Use the negation test: if negating a choice breaks the argument, it is the assumption.

Model: I do -> we do -> you do · application-first (explanations revealed after an attempt) · Rosenshine (daily review), Archer (I-do/we-do/you-do), SPOV2 application-first.

1Opening — retrieval & bridge

Builds on: Telling an argument's conclusion apart from its evidence.

Do Now · from memory, no notes

No notes. Answer from memory in 4 minutes.

  1. In 'Sales rose after we added music, so music boosts sales,' what is the conclusion?Music boosts sales.
  2. What is an assumption?An unstated premise the argument needs to be true.
  3. Negate: 'The two groups were similar in all other respects.'The two groups differed in some other relevant respect.

Retrieval starter (5 min). Recalls conclusion-vs-evidence and negation, the two tools an assumption question rests on.
If students struggle: If they cannot find the conclusion, have them ask 'what is the author trying to convince me of?' first.

Prior-knowledge bridge

You can already find a conclusion and its evidence. An assumption question asks for the missing link between them - the fact the author never states but silently relies on. Find the gap, and the assumption is what bridges it.

Learning intention

By the end you can find the gap between evidence and conclusion and state the assumption that closes it, confirming it with the negation test.

You'll know you've got it when:

  • I can name the conclusion and the evidence in one sentence each.
  • I can describe the gap the argument leaps over.
  • I can use the negation test to confirm the required assumption.

2I do — worked example

Watch the reasoning modeled end to end before you try.

I DO · worked examplemedium
A company will cut its shipping costs by moving its warehouse closer to the highway, because trucks will then spend less time in city traffic. The argument depends on which of the following assumptions?
  • ATime spent in city traffic is a meaningful part of the trucks' current shipping costs.
  • BThe new warehouse location is larger than the current one.
  • CThe company's competitors also ship by truck.
  • DHighway tolls are lower than city parking fees.
  • EThe company ships more products in summer than in winter.

Think-aloud

  1. Conclusion: shipping costs will fall. Evidence: less time in city traffic.
  2. Gap: does traffic time actually drive costs? The argument assumes it does.
  3. Negation test: if traffic time is a negligible cost, moving saves nothing - argument collapses.
  4. So A is the needed assumption; the others are irrelevant to the cost link.

Correct: A. The conclusion (lower shipping costs) rests on cutting traffic time; that only helps if traffic time is actually a meaningful cost driver. Negate A - traffic time is negligible - and the savings vanish, so A is required.

Takeaway. An assumption bridges the evidence to the conclusion; negate it and the argument should fall apart.

3We do — guided practice

Scaffolds and immediate feedback are one click away. Fade them as you gain confidence.

we_1 · we do (guided)medium
A school will raise its graduation rate by giving every student a personal laptop, since students will be able to complete assignments more easily. This argument assumes that
  • Adifficulty completing assignments is a significant reason students currently fail to graduate.
  • Blaptops are cheaper than textbooks.
  • Cteachers prefer digital assignments to paper ones.
  • Dstudents do not already own computers at home.
  • Ethe school's internet connection is reliable.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. What is the conclusion, and what is the single piece of evidence for it?
  2. Ask: for easier assignments to raise graduation, what must be true about why students don't graduate?
  3. Try the negation test on each choice.
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Right - the plan works only if assignment difficulty is actually holding students back.
If incorrect: The other choices may be nice to know, but negating them leaves the argument standing; negating A destroys it.

Answer: A. The plan only raises graduation if assignment difficulty is a real cause of non-graduation; otherwise easier assignments change nothing. Negating A breaks the link.

we_2 · we do (guided)medium
A city will reduce littering in its parks by adding more trash cans, because people litter when a trash can is not within easy reach. The argument relies on the assumption that
  • Aa shortage of nearby trash cans is a substantial cause of the current littering.
  • Bthe new trash cans will be emptied every day.
  • Clittering is worse in parks than on city streets.
  • Dresidents have complained about the litter.
  • Ethe parks are busiest on weekends.
Scaffold hints (peek only if stuck)
  1. Separate the recommendation from its stated reason.
  2. The reason names one cause of littering - the argument bets that cause is the main one.
  3. Which choice, if false, sinks the plan?
Immediate feedback & explanation

If correct: Exactly - the fix assumes the diagnosed cause is a real, substantial one.
If incorrect: Focus on what MUST be true for 'more cans' to cut littering, not on details that merely sound relevant.

Answer: A. Adding cans reduces littering only if 'no can nearby' is really why people litter; if they litter for other reasons, more cans won't help. Negate A and the plan fails.

4You do — independent application

Application-first (SPOV2): commit to an answer, then reveal the explanation. Log every miss.

you_1 · you do (independent)medium

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A publisher will increase magazine subscriptions by adding a weekly puzzle section, reasoning that readers enjoy puzzles. The argument assumes that
  • Aenough potential subscribers value a puzzle section enough to subscribe because of it.
  • Bthe puzzles will be written by a famous puzzle designer.
  • Cthe magazine currently has no games of any kind.
  • Dpuzzle sections are cheaper to produce than articles.
  • Ereaders spend more time on puzzles than on articles.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. Subscriptions rise only if the puzzle section actually moves people to subscribe; general enjoyment of puzzles is not enough. Negating A removes the reason subscriptions would grow.

Application-first — attempt before revealing

A restaurant will boost weekday lunch profits by offering a faster express menu, since many office workers skip lunch out because they lack time. This argument depends on the assumption that
  • Aa meaningful number of these workers would buy lunch if service were faster.
  • Bthe express menu will use the same ingredients as the regular menu.
  • Cthe restaurant is located near several offices.
  • Dcompeting restaurants do not offer express menus.
  • Eoffice workers prefer hot meals to cold ones.
Reveal explanation (after your attempt)

Answer: A. The profit gain depends on time-pressed workers actually buying once service is faster; if they would still not buy, the express menu adds nothing. Negation of A breaks the plan.

5Mastery check

A topic is mastered at >=85% correct on new/held-out-style questions across >=2 spaced sessions with delay (PRD Section 9.2). Flashcard recall alone is not mastery; application under mixed, delayed conditions is the bar.

Threshold 0.85 · spaced sessions ≥ 2
